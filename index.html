Now

I’m building Semiqlassical, where I translate research instincts into tools people can actually rely on.

My focus is data, modeling, and the human factors of decision-making: building systems that behave well under pressure, expose clear feedback loops, and remain useful outside the lab.

Attryx

Attryx is our first product at Semiqlassical: a human-inspectable computational workbench for frontier research.

We are building it for workflows where the path from input to result matters as much as the answer. Code, AI-assisted steps, diagnostics, tables, and results should remain visible enough for people to examine, share, and build on top of.

That matters especially for the next generation of researchers, from college students and undergraduates to graduate students and early technical teams, who need AI to expand their thinking without hiding the process that makes scientific judgment possible.

About Me

I turn noisy signals into decisions.

Trained in chemical engineering and neuroscience, I build measurement-driven ML systems that separate signal from artifact and make those systems reliable in the real world. My toolkit evolved from statistics to machine learning, neural-network-based modeling, and AI agents.

I earned my PhD in Neuroscience at Washington University in St. Louis (advisor: Dr. Daniel Kerschensteiner; thesis: "Dendritic Processing and Efficient Coding in the Retina"). In the Kerschensteiner Lab, my work spans multiphoton imaging, multi-electrode array analysis, and neural-network modeling of visual computation.

Recently, I developed a query-based neural activity prediction model (qNAP) designed to generalize across neurons by disentangling neural descriptors, including receptive-field location and cell-type structure, with the goal of reducing data requirements while improving interpretability and generalization.

As a founder, I bring an engineering bias to research and product: measurable outcomes, pragmatic iteration, and systems that still work under constraints. I build alongside quantum-computing scientist Dr. Avah Banerjee, and we care about helping capable people move faster from ideas to working results.

Earlier, I was a Visiting Scholar in the Parvizi Lab at Stanford University and a Research Assistant at the Institute of Cognitive Neuroscience, National Central University in Taiwan.

My undergraduate training was in Chemical Engineering at National Taiwan University, where my thesis focused on photoactive catalytic reactions.
