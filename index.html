About Me

I turn noisy signals into decisions.

Trained in chemical engineering and neuroscience, I build measurement‑driven ML systems that separate signal from artifact—and make those systems reliable in the real world. My toolkit evolved from statistics → ML → neural‑network‑based ML → AI agents.

I earned my PhD in Neuroscience at Washington University in St. Louis (advisor: Dr. Daniel Kerschensteiner; thesis: "Dendritic Processing and Efficient Coding in the Retina"). In the Kerschensteiner Lab, my work spans multiphoton imaging, multi‑electrode array analysis, and neural‑network modeling of visual computation.

Recently, I developed a query‑based neural activity prediction model (qNAP) designed to generalize across neurons by disentangling neural descriptors (e.g., receptive‑field location and cell‑type structure), with the goal of reducing data requirements while improving interpretability and generalization.

As a founder, I bring an engineering bias to research and product: measurable outcomes, pragmatic iteration, and systems that still work under constraints. I get to build alongside quantum‑computing scientist Dr. Avah Banerjee, and we care about one thing above all—helping capable people move faster from ideas to working results.

Earlier, I was a Visiting Scholar in the Parvizi Lab at Stanford University and a Research Assistant at the Institute of Cognitive Neuroscience, National Central University in Taiwan.

Outside of work, it’s Avah, Mickey (our Chihuahua), and whatever adventure he decides we’re taking.
